Countries

London is one of the most internationally diverse marathons in the world. Raw counts are dominated by GBR, so everything here is normalised — average times, overperformance vs the global field, and which nations produce disproportionately fast runners.

Countries · Diversity
218
Countries represented

Runners from 218 distinct nations have completed the London Marathon across 2014–2026. GBR accounts for ~81% of finishers; the remaining 19% span every inhabited continent.

4:32
Global average finish time

The mean finish across all 530,000 runners. Country analysis below uses this as the baseline — every figure shows how far a country's runners sit above or below it.

Countries · Average Finish Time

European Runners Dominate the Fast End

Among countries with at least 200 runners, Spain leads the field by a clear margin — an average finish of 3:55, more than 37 minutes faster than the global mean. Norway, Portugal, Poland, and France round out the top five.

The pattern reflects the culture of club running and competitive road racing across western and central Europe, where participation skews heavily towards trained runners rather than charity or first-time participants.

Minimum 200 runners. GBR excluded to prevent host-nation size from dominating the visual scale.

Countries · vs Global Average

Who Finishes Faster — and by How Much

Each bar shows how far a country's average finish sits from the global mean of 4:32. Blue = faster than average. Red = slower.

The Philippines (+36 min), Malaysia (+30 min), and Indonesia (+30 min) sit furthest below average — their runner populations in London are predominantly first-timers or charity runners, which shifts the average sharply. Spain's −37 min lead is the widest overperformance in the dataset.

Countries · Elite Representation

Which Countries Punch Above Their Weight in Sub-3:00

5.2% of all finishers run sub-3:00 (the dashed red line). Countries above that line over-represent in the elite field relative to their participation share.

Norway leads at 13.3% — more than 2.6× the field average — followed closely by Israel (12.1%) and Spain (11.3%). Ireland, with 8,261 runners, still manages 9.4% sub-3:00, nearly twice the global rate. This reflects a strong domestic running culture and high club participation among Irish entrants.

Latvia's presence (10.0%) is a notable entry: a small but highly competitive cohort of club runners entering via international slots.

Countries · The Elite Field
KEN & ETH
Own the top 20 fastest finishes

All 20 of the fastest individual finishes in the dataset — every one — belong to a runner from Kenya or Ethiopia. Kenya holds the record: Kelvin Kiptum's 2:01:25 in 2023.

35%
Of elite runners from KEN or ETH

182 of 521 elite-field runners across 2014–2026 came from Kenya or Ethiopia — more than any other pair of nations. GBR has the most total appearances as the host nation.

Why Kenya Doesn't Appear in the Mass Analysis

The earlier charts — fastest countries, vs global average, elite overindex — are all drawn from the mass-start field. Kenya has fewer than 100 mass-start entrants in the dataset, below the 100-runner threshold for the analysis charts, so they are absent there.

The elite field is a separate event. Kenya's 90 elite runners averaged 2:14:30 and posted the single fastest finish in the dataset (Kiptum, 2:01:25). Ethiopia's 92 runners averaged 2:15:01. GBR leads on appearances — 173 — because the host nation is allocated additional elite places.
